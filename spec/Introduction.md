# THE MAIL CLUB

What this is and why I'm building it.


## THE PROBLEM

Most of the ways we meet people are broken in the same way: they
optimize for performance. You show up at a networking event and
pitch yourself. You post on social media and curate an image. You
join a group chat and figure out what to say that sounds right.
The common thread is that you're always shaping yourself to an
audience. And when you do that, you're not really expressing
yourself — you're managing an impression.

I wanted to build something where the structure itself makes
authenticity the path of least resistance. Not by asking people
to "be authentic" — that's just another performance — but by
removing the conditions that make performance possible in the
first place.


## HOW IT WORKS

The mail club runs in seasons. Each season is about a month long.
Here's what happens.

You sign up for a season. During the creation period — roughly two
weeks — you make something. A letter, a drawing, a collage, a
journal entry, pressed flowers, whatever you want, as long as it
fits in a Nova Poshta document envelope. This is your
self-expression. The catch: you don't know who's going to receive
it.

That's the core of the whole thing. Because you don't know your
recipient, you can't tailor what you make to them. You can't
perform for them. You can't worry about what they'll think of you
specifically. The only thing left to draw from is yourself.

Once the ready-confirm deadline passes, the organizer runs the
assignments. You get a name, a phone number, and a Nova Poshta
branch. You go ship your mail. A few days later, someone else's self-expression
arrives for you.

Then we all meet up.

At the meetup, we go around the circle. Each person shares one
specific thing about what they received — something that struck
them. The sender is named openly. By the end of the circle,
every person's creation has been spoken about, and every person
has heard how their work landed.

After the circle, you find your sender and your recipient and
talk. Then the structure dissolves and people just hang out.


## WHY THE DESIGN CHOICES MATTER

The single-recipient model means no comparisons. You didn't send
your work into a room of fifteen people who all compare it. One
person received it, and they gave it their full attention. That's
rare. That's the opposite of a feed.

The loop topology — within each cohort, every person sends to one
and receives from one, forming a single unbroken chain. No cliques.
No subgroups. Everyone in the cohort is woven into one fabric. The
graph itself is the equality.

The physical medium matters. A handwritten letter has weight. It
required someone to sit down, make something with their hands,
walk to a post office, and send it. You can feel that effort when
you hold it. A digital file can't do this.

The blind creation matters most. When you remove the recipient
from the equation at the moment of creation, people default to
honesty. Not because they're told to, but because there's nothing
else to optimize for.


## HOW SEASONS CONNECT

Each season, the pool is reshuffled into new groups. You won't be
with the same people twice in a row. Over multiple seasons, you
encounter more and more of the community through their
self-expression. The connections form slowly, through repeated
acts of creation and reception, not through small talk.

The algorithm that assigns recipients is aware of existing social
connections. If you already know someone, you're less likely to be
paired with them. The system pushes people toward strangers, not
toward comfort.


## WHAT I'M BUILDING TOWARD

This is a community, not a product. It starts in Kyiv, with one
group of about a dozen people. If it works — if the meetup
produces the kind of connection I think it will — it scales by
adding parallel groups, not by making groups bigger. Each group
stays intimate. The format stays the same. Parallel cohorts are a
future path, contingent on the team growing beyond a solo operator;
the format holds, but the operational capacity has to grow first.

The app exists to handle logistics: sign-ups, assignments, SMS
reminders, delivery confirmations. Everything that matters — the
creation, the sending, the receiving, the meeting — happens
offline, between people, with physical objects.

The goal is manufactured intimacy that becomes real intimacy. The
structure does the hard work of breaking the barrier of not
knowing people. Once that barrier is broken — once you've
expressed yourself to strangers and heard strangers express
themselves to you, several times over — the connections can form
on their own. The scaffolding becomes unnecessary. But without
it, most people never start.
